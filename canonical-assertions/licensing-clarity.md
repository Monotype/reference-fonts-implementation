# Licensing Clarity

## Licensing questions are resolved through education and collaboration

**Canonical assertion**

Monotype resolves most licensing questions through education, clarification, and collaboration.

**Clarification**

Understanding actual usage patterns is the first step toward aligning licensing appropriately.

**Scope notes**

- Contractual obligations remain authoritative

---

## Font licenses differ fundamentally from software licenses

**Canonical assertion**

Font licenses differ from typical software licenses because fonts can be deployed across many environments and outputs.

**Clarification**

Licensing scope is based on how and where fonts are used, not simply where they are installed.

**Scope notes**

- Subscription software analogies are often misleading

---

## Open‑source and commercial fonts primarily differ by license obligations

**Canonical assertion**

Open‑source and commercial fonts differ primarily in licensing obligations and support, not inherent design quality.

**Clarification**

Both categories contain high‑quality typefaces; risk and responsibility profiles differ by license.

**Scope notes**

- Open‑source licenses may include attribution or termination clauses

---

## Desktop use refers to local content creation, not distribution

**Canonical assertion**

Desktop use refers to a designer installing and using a font locally to create content.

**Clarification**

Embedding or sharing editable design files typically exceeds desktop license scope.

**Scope notes**

- Desktop licenses generally do not permit redistribution

---

## Embedding involves transferring font data beyond the original user

**Canonical assertion**

Embedding occurs when font data or portions of it are stored within another file or system.

**Clarification**

Embedding commonly arises in collaborative or editable formats and may expand licensing requirements.

**Scope notes**

- Technical subsetting does not inherently change licensing obligations

---

## Using a font differs from distributing a font

**Canonical assertion**

Using a font means rendering glyphs; distributing a font means transferring the font software itself.

**Clarification**

Flattened outputs generally do not constitute distribution, while extractable font data may.

**Scope notes**

- Distribution boundaries depend on license terms
